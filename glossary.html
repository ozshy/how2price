Advertising

A costly marketing tool by which firms obtain get new customers or old customers to repurchase the advertised brand(s). The economist literature classifies advertising into two categories:  

Persuasive-advertising which boosts the demand for the brand(s) by simply "persuading" more consumers to buy the product and/or persuading consumers to buy more units. Persuasive advertising sometimes relies on creating an image for potential consumers to identify with.

Informative-advertising that may provide information about products' characteristics, usefulness, operation, warranty, as well as specific prices. One commonly-used type of informative-advertising is called comparison-advertising by which the advertised brand is directly compared with specific characteristics of a particular competing brand.  Comparison advertising in particular, and information advertising in general, tend to make brands more differentiated, thereby weakening price competition among sellers in the industry.

Bundling

Bundling, also known as quantity-discount, refers to the sale of a package containing several (more than one) units of the same product. This bundle is priced as a package and not per-unit. Bundling involves selling of several units of the same good for a single price; in this respect it differs from tying which involves packaging units of different goods.

Capacity constraint

By capacity constraint we refer to a situation where a firm cannot produce beyond a certain output level.  We generally denote by Qmax the maximum number of units the firm can produce.  Capacity constraint models are also useful to describe firms or stores that are "stuck" with inventories with a low marginal delivery cost.

When a firm's profit maximizing output level exceeds the capacity level we say that the capacity constraint is binding.  When a firm's profit-maximizing levels is below the capacity level, we say that the capacity constraint is non-binding (meaning that the firm can ignore its capacity constraint when solving for its profit-maximizing output or price levels.

In services sectors (as well as in some other highly-perishable goods sectors), capacity constraint leads to overbooking of customers when consumers have positive probability of no-shows. This is because of the high opportunity cost of underutilized capacity.

Concentration measures

A concentration index is a number reflecting the degree of monopoly power captured by imperfectly-competitive firms (sellers).  The highest degree of concentration is associated with a single seller (monopoly) holding 100% of the market.  The degree of concentration declines with an increase in the number of active firms (sellers), and/or a change towards more equally-divided market shares.

The most-commonly used concentration indexes are:  The 4-largest-firms concentration index (0 < I4 < 100%) which sums the market shares  (in %) of the 4 largest firms.  The Herfindahl-Hirshman index (0 < IHH < 10,000 = 1002)  which sums the squares of the market shares (in %) of active firms.

 Concentration indexes are used by the regulating authorities to evaluate the market consequences of a (horizontal) merger of two firms in the same industry.

Cost of production

Let q denote the production (output level) of a single firm.  That is, q denotes the number of units produced.  Economic theory classifies cost of production into four components:

  1. Marginal cost of production, denoted by MC(q).  The marginal cost is the change in production cost associated with a small increase in the production level (small could be one unit).
  2. Average cost of production, denoted by AC(q).  The average cost is the cost-per-unit of production, obtained by dividing total cost by the output level.
  3. Fixed cost of production, denoted by F.  The fixed cost is the sum of all costs that are independent of the production level (i.e., costs that must be borne even if no output is produced).
  4. Sunk cost.  Same as fixed cost, but this cost cannot be recouped in case that the firm ceases to exist.  For example, cost of advertising and taxes paid.

Critical mass

Consider a service by which each consumer benefits from an increase in the total number of consumers subscribed to the same service. At a given subscription price, p, the critical mass at p is the minimum number of subscribed consumers that will guarantee that more consumers will subscribed to the same service in the future.

Example include telecommunication services where no consumer will ever subscribe knowing that no one else subscribes.  For example, no consumer will subscribe to a phone or an Internet network knowing that no one else will since no communication can be made.  However, at any given subscription price, there is a threshold subscription volume beyond which more consumers will subscribe simply because they can interact with a "sufficient" number of other subscribed consumers.

Critical mass has also social implication. When organizing  party, one has to secure a minimum number of people who are willing to commit to participate. Once this critical mass is secure, it is clear that everybody else will also show up for the party.

Demand function

A demand function summarizes and observed relationship between quantity demanded by consumers and given market prices.  Thus, the demand function captures the maximum amount consumers are willing to pay at any given price.  Let p denote a given market price and Q aggregate quantity demanded in a given market, this relationship is summarized by a function Q(p ). 

For historical reasons, economists tend to write the inverse demand function rather than the direct demand function in the form of p(Q ).  Thus, the inverse demand function captures the maximum price consumers are willing to pay to by any given quantity.

An inverse demand function is said to be linear if it takes the form p = a - b Q, where a > 0 and b > 0.  For example, p = 12 - 3 Q, in which case the direct demand function would be  Q = 4 - p / 3.

A demand function is called exponential or a constant-elasticity demand function if it takes the form of  Q = a p-e, where a > 0.  The estimated parameter e < 0 is the demand elasticity which happens to be constant (i.e., does not vary when prices and quantities) for this demand function.

Whereas ordinary demand functions are downward sloping (in the price-quantity space), meaning that a fall in price tends to increase quantity demanded, the demand for network services and products has a unique feature where the inverse demand increases (rather than decreases) at low aggregate consumption levels reflecting the fact that consumers are willing to pay a higher (subscription) prices for more popular services .

Demand functions are estimated using regression equations that catalog observed prices, quantity demanded, and perhaps other variables, such as advertising and income levels, for the purpose of extracting the parameters charactering the demand relationship (for example, intercepts and slopes).

Differentiated products

A strict definition of differentiated products (differentiated brands) refers to brands that consumers distinguish among the different producers of the different brands. In fact, the brands may or may not be physically different, but even if they are not physically different, competition among sellers of otherwise identical products would lead firms to engage in heavy advertising that turns homogeneous products into differentiated brands.

Markets for differentiated brands are generally characterize by heavy advertising attempting to associate an image with the advertised brand, consumer switching costs, and reputation effects.  All these factors weaken price competition which enable competing firms to exert some degree of monopoly power over their customers.

Elasticity of demand

The price elasticity at a given price-quantity pair (p,Q) , is defined by the ratio: Percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price.  

At a given price, the price elasticity, denoted by e, is a number between zero and minus infinity.  For example, if e = -2, a 10% increase in price will reduce quantity demanded by 20%.  If e = -1,  we say that the demand elasticity is unity, in which case a 10% increase in price will result in a 10% fall in quantity demanded.

On a given demand function, elasticity varies with the price (quantity), so elasticity is a function and not a number.  However, there is a class of demand functions called constant-elasticity demand functions where the elasticity remains the same at every price-quantity pair satisfying this demand function.

Forecasting time series

In case there is a good reason to believe that an economic variable (such as quantity demanded at a given price) follows a single trend, forecasting by extrapolating a time series tend to be an easy method for predicting the economic variable. A time series is a list of realizations at each given point in time (e.g., daily, weekly, monthly, or yearly observations).

The naive one-step forecasting is asigning the latest realization as the best predictor for next period's observation. A moving-average forecast would be the average of some past observations. For example, a 4-period moving average forecast would involve averaging the past 4 observations as the predictor of next-period realization.

The problem with the naive forecast is that it is subjected to large errors caused by seasonal fluctuations or some unforseen events. Therefore, a common forecasting technique involves smoothing in order to mitigate some of the more volatile short-term fluctuations. There are various methods of smoothing. The method used here is called exponential smoothing which turns out to be a weighted average of the naive and the moving-average forecasts. 

Forecasting demand by extrapolating a time series differs significantly from estimating a demand function utilizing regression techniques. A time series extrapolation assumes that the demand follows a certain trend over time. However, a regression technique attemts to estimate the precise relationship between quantity and prices. Of course, more sophisticated regression techniques would involve regressing quantity demanded on past prices and quantities.

Homogeneous products

A strict definition of homogeneous products would mean brands in which consumers do not bother to investigate if and whether there differences between the competing products or a services. In particular, consumers do not inquire about the names of the producers in these markets.  In such markets, there are no leading brand names, and very little advertising (if any) is observed.

Price competition among firms/stores selling homogenous products or services is the most intense, and often leads to marginal cost pricing since consumers always pick the cheapest brand, and do not remain loyal to any particular seller.  This leads firms to invest in heavy advertising in order to turn homogeneous products into differentiated products (differentiated brands).  For example, gas station engage in heavy advertising in order to persuade customers that their gasoline is special and differs from the competing brands (despite the fact that there are no differences between the different brands (for the same octane level).

Merger guidelines

Mergers are classified into three types: Horizontal merger when firms in the same industry, producing identical or similar products and selling in the same geographical market, merge.  Vertical merger when a firm producing an intermediate good (or a factor of production) merges with a firm producing the final good that uses this
intermediate good.  Conglomerate merger occurs when firms producing less related products merge under the same
ownership.

Merger guidelines constitute suggestions to regulating authorities (FTC, DOJ, in the U.S.) when to consider challenging a horizontal merger.  Among other consideration, the regulator computes pre- and post-merger concentration indexes for this industry.

Network Services and Products

Network goods refer to services and products in which consumers' willingness to pay increases the total number of all consumers subscribing to the same service (or buying the same product).

Telecommunication services are regarded as network services since consumers' utility is enhanced with an increase in the number of other consumers with who each subscriber can communication. For example, the demand for fax machines and the demand for electronic mail services.

The inverse demand function for network services and goods may be increasing at early subscription stages (low aggregate consumption levels) reflecting the fact that consumers are willing to pay a higher (subscription) prices for more popular services .

Overbooking

The practice of overbooking means selling tickets (making reservations) beyond the capacity of the service provider. This practice reduces expected underutilization of capacity in the presence of positive probability of no-shows. Overbooking is a strategy related to a more general practice called advance booking of orders (ABO).  Advance booking is essential in industries where demand is expected to exceeds the capacity of service provider. Overbooking is also related to whether the service provider (producer, in general) makes refundable or non-refundable bookings of orders (e.g., selling refundable or non-refundable tickets).

Those consumers who make reservations and show up for the service are called survivals. If the number of survivals (the number of those who show up for the service) exceeds capacity, the excess is oversales (oversold customers). If the number of survivals is less than capacity, the difference between capacity and survivals is spoilage (or, underutilized capacity).

The service provider's decision problem is to maximize expected payoff which includes the cost of both spoilage and oversales. Oversales result in a wide variety of costs to the service provider, which include goodwill cost (angry customers), penalty costs, and having to refund the ticket price to consumes who are denied service (bumped consumers).

Finally, overbooking is heavily practiced in the airline industry. Some flights are overbooked with more than 30% of seating capacity. The FAA issues direct guidelines which limits overbooking, and direct the airline how a passenger who is denied service must be compensated.

Peak-load pricing

Peak-load pricing applies to markets for products and services of the following characteristics:

  1. Fluctuations in demand according to time periods (called seasons).
  2. The product/service is non-storable meaning that it cannot be purchased in one season and used in another season.
  3. Production of this product or a service requires a large investment in capacity.

Examples: (a) Production of electricity requires a large investment in capacity in the form of generators.  Electricity is non-storable, and demand fluctuates between day and night, and weekdays and weekends.  (b) Air travel requires airline companies to invest in aircraft capacity.  Travel is non-storable and demand varies between winter and summer.

Time lengths:  Time is divided into periods.  Periods are devided into seasons.  For example, we can identify periods with years, where each year is divided into two seasons called winter and summer.

Cost of production: There are three types of costs:

  1. Marginal cost of operation, denoted by oc (operating cost).  This is the cost of producing/providing one additional unit.  For example, producing one additional kilowatt/hour of electricity, cost of ticketing and boarding one additional passenger on a plane.
  2. Marginal cost of capacity, denoted by cc (capacity cost). 
  3. Fixed (or sunk) cost, denoted by F.  This cost is independent of the level of production.

Seasons, time periods, and the allocation of production costs:  Suppose that there are only two seasons (e.g., winter/summer, or day/night).  Then, we assume that additional unit of capacity (cost denoted by cc) is usable in both seasons.  That is, capacity is purchased only once and lasts for two seasons.  In contrast, operating cost (denoted by oc) must be paid each season.

Suppose now that the two seasons are repeated n periods (e.g., winter and summer are repeated n years).  Then, assuming that capacity lasts for the entire n periods, the capacity cost becomes cc/n per period (constisting of two seasons).

Refundable and non-refundable bookings

Advance booking of orders has two advantages: (a) Consumers (buyers) can ensure that the service provider (or seller, in general) will provide the service when it is scheduled to be delivered. (b) For sellers, advance booking is used to estimate the actual demand at the time of delivery.

One problem with advance booking is that customers may not show up at the delivery time. If the orders are fully-refundable (e.g., refundable tickets),  then the cost of no-shows (stemming from under-utilized capacity), are borne by the service provider (seller, in general). In contrast, if the bookings are non-refundable (e.g., non-refundable tickets), the costs of no-shows are borne by consumers only. This implies that service providers should price refundable tickets higher than non-refundable tickets. If only fully-refundable tickets are sold, the seller may exercise an overbooking strategy (if it is legal).

The service provider has three options:
(a) Selling non-refundable tickets.
(b) Selling fully-refundable tickets.
(c) Selling, both, refundable and non-refundable tickets. Under this booking strategy, consumers who are likely to show up purchase the discounted non-refundable tickets, whereas consumers who are less likely to show up (less reliable consumers) purchase fully-refundable tickets.

Finally, refundability options are heavily practiced in the airline industry. Full-fare, Business, and First-class tickets tend to be fully-refundable. In contrast, several types of discounted tickets are non-refundable.

Regression (Statistical estimation)

Regression is a statistic technique of fitting a functional relationship among economic variables by cataloging the observed variables, and then utilizing well-known formulas to extract the exogenous parameters of the functional relationship.

In microeconomics, regressions are heavily used to estimate demand functions by cataloging observed prices and quantity demanded, thereby obtaining the parameters characterizing the functional relationship

The most common used method is known as ordinary least squares (a method which minimizes the sum of squared errors).  Simple linear regressions refer to fitting of linear functions (such as linear demand functions).  Exponential demand functions are estimated by cataloging the natural logs of the observed variables and utilizing linear regressions (double-logarithmic estimation).  You are welcome to use the regression pages given on this site.  In fact, all the demand functions used on this site can be estimated on the sites' regression pages.  However, you should also be aware of the fact that almost all spreadsheet programs provide regression analyses.  Thus, if you have data sets containing a large number of observations you may be better off saving this data as a spreadsheet file and run the regressions utilizing the same software.  The popular spreadsheet programs can also draw the regression lines for you together with the observation you catalog.

If your demand observations consist of a time series that follows a single trend (say, because there are no significant changes in the market price), you may want to consider utilizing a forecasting technique extrapolating a time series.

Switching costs

Switching costs refers to the total cost associated with using a brand different from the brand which was initially purchased.  Switching costs could be explicit (such as the time it takes to learn how to operate a new brand), or implicit (such as loss of long-term relationship with service providers when choosing a different service, or emotional stress in getting used to rules of the new service provider).

Examples for switching costs in various industries are: Loss of frequent-flyer mileage when switching airline carriers.  Learning how to use a computer operating on a different operating system.  Getting salaries and paid bills via a newly-opened account with a competing bank.

Just like differentiated products , switching costs are known to weaken price competition since consumers are unlikely to switch brand unless the competing bank is sufficiently (price-wise) discounted to compensate the consumer for bearing the switching costs. To solve for market equilibrium prices under switching costs one has to utilize the undercut-proof equilibrium concept.

Tying

Tying refers to the sale of a package containing different goods for a single price (rather than pricing each good separately).  Tying could be made explicitly (e.g., assorted fruits, assorted tools, hardware tied with software, etc.), or it can be made implicitly (e.g., buy one package and get a "free" T-shirt).  Tying differs from bundling which refers to a package containing several units of the same good.

Undercut-proof equilibrium prices

Undercut-proof prices are equilibrium prices in which no seller would find it profitable to lower his price below any rival's price and subsidize the rivals consumer switching costs in order to grab the rival's customers to switch brands. Undercut-proof equilibrium is used to solve for equilibrium prices when consumers bear switching costs of switching from the brand they purchased before to a different brand.
